Summary

As agentic AI systems move from experimental tools to widely deployed consumer and enterprise infrastructure, questions of hallucination, misconduct, and cascading errors increasingly implicate product liability, consumer protection, and institutional accountability. Rather than presuming the need for sweeping new legislation, this project examines how existing U.S. legal and policy frameworks can be leveraged and adapted to govern advanced agent systems at scale.

§ 01

Background

In 2025, 46 states passed 159 laws related to artificial intelligence — a 48% increase from the prior year. These statistics, coupled with the American Bar Association's call for more sensible definitions of AI systems and Congress's ongoing definitional efforts, signal a clear need: a common language for a rapidly evolving technology.

Agentic AI systems are projected to produce approximately $2.9 trillion in economic value between 2025 and 2030, yet our newly minted governance tools cannot perfectly capture the risks associated with increasingly autonomous AI agents.

Consider Mata v. Avianca, Inc., which found that submitting AI-generated "gibberish" constitutes "subjective bad faith" sufficient for sanctions under FRCP Rule 11. While suited to that instance, this framework does not map cleanly onto systems involving AI agents acting as proxy counsel, research attorneys, or law clerks.

Similarly, the Texas Responsible Artificial Intelligence Governance Act (TRAIGA), effective January 1, 2026, spans more than twenty-five pages yet contains no explicit reference to "agents" or agentic systems — reflecting a broader tendency to regulate AI by sector, risk tier, or output rather than by autonomous functionality or delegated decision-making authority.

§ 02

Hypothesis

If existing legislative and policy frameworks can be applied to certain AI agent use cases, policymakers will be better positioned in the short run to rely on established enforcement mechanisms while still being able to identify gaps that require targeted legislative reform to protect consumers.
§ 03

Methodology

The project unfolds across four structured phases, moving from legal baseline through taxonomy development, regulatory overlay, and final gap analysis.

I

Phase I

Legal & Case Law Mapping
Comprehensive review of federal and state statutes, administrative rulemakings, and judicial opinions to establish what the law already governs.

II

Phase II

Functional Taxonomy
A structured taxonomy of AI behaviors based on functional outputs — not model architecture — codifying what "agentic behavior" means in practice.

III

Phase III

Policy & Regulatory Overlay
Mapping the taxonomy onto existing statutes, rulemakings, EOs, NIST standards, and pending proposals to produce a coverage matrix.

IV

Phase IV

Gap Analysis & Recommendations
Synthesizing findings into a gap analysis distinguishing enforcement failure, definitional ambiguity, and true legal absence.

Functional Taxonomy Categories

Phase II will develop at minimum seven categories of agentic behavior for regulatory mapping:

Regulatory Domain Coverage Status Notes

§ 04

Theory of Change

The intended audience consists of members of the Congressional AI Caucus and their staff, as well as federal bodies such as NIST. Both Congress and NIST have expressed interest in receiving input on the governance of AI agents — NIST's January 2026 Request for Information on security considerations for AI agents makes this especially timely.

After engaging with this project, the audience should be better oriented around what legal and policy frameworks currently exist and where meaningful gaps remain. The goal is not to prescribe immediate legislative overhaul, but to provide a clearer foundation for evaluating when existing authorities are sufficient and when targeted intervention may be warranted.

Ideally, this work serves as a segue into further research, more structured institutional inquiry, and ultimately more coherent conversations about what should — and realistically could — be done next.

§ 05

Why the Frontier Security Team

This project fits comfortably within the body of work produced by the Frontier Security team for several reasons. It aligns with the normative and policy-oriented framing reflected in prior publications on cyber defense policy. It extends the team's prior exploration of societal integration as outlined in its AI Governance field guide, applying similar principles to the emerging governance challenges posed by agentic systems. Finally, the project is timely — recent NIST interest in standards and security considerations for AI agents underscores the need for structured, policy-relevant analysis.

§ 06

Potential Limitations

  • The proposed taxonomy could be misaligned with what the intended audience is most interested in addressing. By the time the project is ready for publication, the priorities of Congress, NIST, or other stakeholders may have shifted.
  • The project's normative framing may not resonate as expected, particularly if definitional or structural governance work is better received when developed within institutions such as NIST or the Executive Office of the President rather than through IAPS.
  • History suggests that legislation and adjudication often proceed without fully settled taxonomies — foundational concepts like personhood, employee status, or vehicle classification have evolved through interpretation and refinement rather than upfront definitional clarity.
  • IAPS itself may not be the most natural institutional home for this type of framework-driven governance work, raising reasonable questions about fit and audience reach.
