Summary

This research maps seven functional categories of AI tools to (a) widely used U.S. non-AI legal and policy frameworks that already govern analogous activity, and (b) AI-era or AI-explicit frameworks now being used by regulators, legislators, and standards bodies. The core insight—emphasized in U.S. enforcement messaging—is that "AI" seldom needs wholly new law: existing consumer protection and civil-rights frameworks already apply when automated systems create unlawful or harmful outcomes.

§ 01

Scope & How to Read This Research

"AI" is used here in the sense of the U.S. statutory definition: a machine-based system that, for human-defined objectives, makes predictions, recommendations, or decisions that influence real or virtual environments.

"Framework" includes: binding statutes and regulations; recurring doctrinal tests from courts (e.g., due process balancing; disparate impact and "less discriminatory alternative" concepts); and widely used governance standards or official guidance (e.g., NIST risk frameworks; sector regulator circulars).

§ 02

Cross-Cutting Frameworks

A small number of "horizontal" frameworks recur across most AI categories because many AI tools ultimately affect (i) consumer transactions, (ii) competition, (iii) protected-class outcomes, or (iv) privacy/security.

Core insight: AI seldom needs a wholly new body of law. Existing consumer protection and civil-rights frameworks already apply when automated systems create unlawful or harmful outcomes — a position explicitly emphasized in U.S. enforcement messaging.

Consumer Protection

Oversight frequently routes through the FTC Act's prohibition on unfair or deceptive acts or practices (Section 5). A core "unfairness" framework is the three-part test: substantial injury; not outweighed by countervailing benefits; not reasonably avoidable by consumers. In consumer finance, UDAAP authority is codified in Dodd-Frank/CFPA (12 U.S.C. §§ 5531, 5536).

Competition & Pricing

The baseline regime remains the Sherman Act (restraints of trade; monopolization), with enforcement framed around consumer harm. Adjacent pricing frameworks include Robinson-Patman (price discrimination) and FTC competition policy under Section 5 "unfair methods of competition."

Civil Rights & Anti-Discrimination

Core recurring structures are disparate treatment and disparate impact. In employment, Title VII and the Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection Procedures (UGESP) provide a long-standing analytical structure; the "four-fifths rule" is explicitly referenced as a screening heuristic. In housing, the Fair Housing Act is central, with the Supreme Court recognizing disparate impact claims and guardrails including consideration of less discriminatory alternatives.

Privacy & Security

U.S. practice is sectoral: HIPAA for certain health data; GLBA for financial institutions; FERPA for education records; and a rapidly expanding state-law landscape for comprehensive consumer privacy and biometrics. Risk-management standards across sectors include NIST's AI RMF (voluntary), NIST's Privacy Framework, and NIST's Cybersecurity Framework 2.0.

§ 05

What Is Being Used in AI Governance Today

U.S. AI governance in 2026 is best described as layered: existing baseline frameworks (consumer protection, civil rights, privacy/security, and sector safety) are being supplemented by more explicit AI governance instruments in government operations, some state statutes, and certain sector regulators.

The federal executive-policy layer: A January 23, 2025 Executive Order ("Removing Barriers to American Leadership in Artificial Intelligence") revoked EO 14110 and directed review/revision of Biden-era AI memoranda. OMB subsequently issued M-25-21 (federal AI use) and M-25-22 (federal AI acquisition), each explicitly rescinding and replacing earlier memos. These matter for private-sector governance because they influence procurement expectations, "minimum practices," and audit/recordkeeping norms that vendors operationalize across customers.

Civil Rights & Consumer Protection EnforcementAgencies emphasize that automated systems are "not an excuse for lawbreaking behavior." Joint statements and technical assistance explicitly apply existing discrimination law to AI tools — visible in enforcement and settlements including biometric/facial recognition contexts.
State & Local LevelColorado's "high-risk AI" obligations (enacted 2024) were delayed to June 30, 2026 via SB25B-004. NYC's AEDT law requires bias audits and notices for automated employment decision tools. California's ADS employment regulations took effect October 1, 2025, clarifying how anti-discrimination law applies to AI in employment decisionmaking.
Sector Safety (Medical Devices)The most mature AI-specific lifecycle governance is in medical devices. FDA maintains an AI/ML software-as-a-medical-device policy track including draft lifecycle management and marketing submission recommendations and "Good Machine Learning Practice" principles.
InsuranceNAIC adopted a model bulletin on insurers' use of AI systems. NYDFS issued a circular letter regarding AI systems and external data in underwriting/pricing — both framed as compliance with existing insurance laws but with AI governance specificity.
Generative Media & DeepfakesThree "AI-era" anchors stand out: (i) Copyright Office registration guidance for AI-generated content; (ii) the Copyright Office multi-part AI report series analyzing digital replicas; (iii) the TAKE IT DOWN Act, requiring platforms to implement notice-and-removal for nonconsensual intimate imagery including AI "digital forgeries," enforced by the FTC.
§ 06

Notes on Gaps, Overlaps & How to Use These Tables

Mappings are functional, not industry-specific

A single deployed system can trigger multiple frameworks at once. A recommender used in credit (targeting + ranking + pricing) can implicate privacy opt-outs, fair lending, and unfairness/deception simultaneously. Interagency joint guidance explicitly frames automated systems as capable of producing unlawful discrimination through data, design, deployment, and governance failures — meaning mapping should consider the full lifecycle, not just the model.

Privacy & biometrics law is largely state-driven

The "most used" answer depends on footprint. NCSL maintains continually updated summaries showing state comprehensive privacy laws and deepfake/deceptive media legislative activity.

Building a repeatable internal compliance method

The most common "bridge" frameworks used in practice across all seven categories are:

Consumer Protection LensUnfairness/deception/UDAAP analysis as a baseline risk screen for any AI-driven consumer-facing practice.
Civil Rights LensDisparate treatment/impact analysis; business necessity; less discriminatory alternatives — applied across categories where AI influences consequential decisions about individuals.
Lifecycle Risk Management LensNIST AI RMF for AI governance, plus NIST CSF and Privacy Framework for security and privacy risk — increasingly referenced in state AI statutes and federal procurement.

Where state law is AI-explicit (e.g., Colorado), those laws often directly operationalize this bridging approach through requirements such as impact assessments, monitoring, transparency, and references to NIST AI RMF and ISO/IEC 42001.
